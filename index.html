About Me

I study the most powerful explosions in the universe, known as gamma-ray bursts. They are brief flashes of gamma-rays known to occur as the result of the collapse of rapidly-rotating massive stars or neutron star mergers. I make predictions for the gamma-ray burst observations that will be made by COSI, an upcoming gamma-ray space telescope and am the technical lead for the COSI gamma-ray burst science group. I also analyze gamma-ray bursts detected by the Fermi Gamma-ray Burst Monitor.

Outside of research, I play harp, dance a partnered social dance called Brazilian zouk, boulder, go for long walks, and care for my many houseplants.

Research

Gamma-Ray Burst from a Black Hole Merging with a Star

The prompt gamma-ray emission of most gamma-ray bursts lasts for no more than several minutes. On July 2, 2025, the Fermi Gamma-ray Burst Monitor detected a gamma-ray burst whose prompt duration was later found to be over 7 hours, making it the longest ever seen. This extreme duration and gamma-ray properties cannot be explained by any known origin of gamma-ray bursts. It is instead explained naturally by a stellar-mass black hole falling into a helium star, and consuming and exploding it from within.
